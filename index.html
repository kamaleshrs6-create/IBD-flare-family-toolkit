🚦 Is My Child Having an IBD Flare? What Should I Do Next?

A guide to recognizing symptoms and taking appropriate action

What is an IBD Flare?

An IBD flare is a period when your child's inflammatory bowel disease becomes more active, causing increased inflammation in the digestive tract. During a flare, symptoms become worse and may require additional treatment or medical attention.

Understanding the signs of a flare and knowing how to respond quickly can help minimize discomfort and prevent complications. Use this guide to assess your child's symptoms and determine the appropriate next steps.

GREEN - Mild Symptoms

  • Mild abdominal discomfort
  • Slightly increased bowel movements
  • Minimal blood or mucus
  • Normal energy levels
  • No fever
Action: Monitor at home, continue medications, ensure hydration

AMBER - Moderate Symptoms

  • Moderate abdominal pain
  • Frequent bowel movements (6-8+)
  • Blood or mucus in stool
  • Reduced appetite
  • Low fever (37.5-38.5°C)
Action: Contact your IBD team for guidance

RED - Severe Symptoms

  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Very frequent stools (8+ per day)
  • Heavy blood loss in stool
  • High fever (>38.5°C)
  • Signs of dehydration
Action: Seek urgent medical help immediately

5 Things NOT to Do During a Flare

Don't eat high-fiber foods

Avoid whole grains, raw vegetables, and high-fiber foods that may irritate

Don't skip hydration

Never reduce fluid intake during flares when dehydration risk is high

Don't stop medications

Continue prescribed treatments unless instructed by your IBD team

Don't delay seeking help

Contact your IBD team early rather than waiting for symptoms to worsen

Don't ignore severe symptoms

Severe pain or bleeding requires immediate medical attention

Understanding Red Flags: Why They Matter

Red flags are warning signs that require urgent medical attention. Recognizing and acting on red flags quickly can prevent serious complications.

Why Severe Bleeding Needs Urgent Review

Heavy bleeding can lead to anemia (low iron), weakness, and fatigue. Persistent or severe bleeding may indicate complications such as ulcers or perforations that require immediate intervention.

Why Dehydration is Dangerous

Dehydration can cause electrolyte imbalances, dizziness, reduced kidney function, and worsening of flare symptoms. In children, dehydration can develop rapidly and may require hospital admission.

Why Severe Pain Should Not Be Ignored

Severe pain may indicate serious complications like bowel obstruction, perforation, or toxic megacolon—all conditions requiring emergency medical intervention to prevent life-threatening situations.

Why High Fever Matters

A high fever (>38.5°C) in combination with IBD symptoms may indicate infection, sepsis, or severe inflammation. This requires urgent medical evaluation to rule out dangerous complications.

Why General Deterioration Requires Action

If your child is becoming increasingly unwell, lethargic, or experiencing multiple worsening symptoms together, this indicates serious flare activity requiring urgent medical intervention.

Trust Your Instincts

You know your child best. If something feels wrong but you're unsure, don't hesitate to contact your IBD team. It's always better to check and be reassured than to delay care.

Trust Your Instincts

As a parent, you have invaluable knowledge of your child's normal patterns and behavior. If you notice something isn't right—even if symptoms seem mild—trust your gut instinct and seek guidance.

If you are unsure about the severity of symptoms or whether to seek help, always err on the side of caution and contact your IBD team. Early communication with your healthcare provider can prevent complications.

When in doubt, reach out. Your concerns are valid and important.
